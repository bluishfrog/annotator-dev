Summary

George knew that he was good enough to win. It wasn’t hard to be confident about it—not when he was looking at his statistics, not when he clicked into his skis, and definitely not when he was racing down the hill so fast, he almost felt like he was flying.

But despite his confidence, despite everyone agreeing that he was overdue for a win, George had been damned to almost-win and never actually-win.

So he thought himself pretty reasonable for despising Dream. Who wouldn’t hate the guy who so often cut his own time down just a few hundreds of a second, who so often snatched the top spot for himself?

No charming smile would change how George felt about his rival. And no earnest compliments, whispered where the press couldn’t hear them. Neither would accepting Dream’s invitation to an after-race-party. Letting himself give into his own impulsivity (definitely not desire!) and kiss Dream before dragging him into the nearby closet—yeah, that for sure wouldn’t change things either.

If George has to guess what is shown underneath his name on television right now, it would be Chasing first win. He hopes it isn’t something stupid like Most podiums without a win. Ever. Or worst of all: Fastest if not for Dream, 33 times. He would really hate that one.

When he pushes out of the start gate in Beaver Creek, the only thing on his mind is how he doesn’t want to end up second to Dream again. It is a stupid thing to think about at this moment, considering how the downhill race in Colorado is one of the most difficult in the world, and he should probably be focusing on racing.

He pushes the thoughts of his one-sided rivalry with the best skier of his generation away as he uses his ski poles to gain speed in the flat starting section of the race, crouching down as low as he can once he picks up enough speed. Ever since the last training run, George has gone over the videos time and time again. He knows exactly where he can still improve, which turns to ski more tightly and where he should let go earlier. And he has memorised the key passage around the Golden Eagle to the very last snow crystal. He’d watched the clip so often that the pixels blurred—until the Golden Eagle lived behind his eyes like a map. He will not let this passage ruin his race again.

George has won the first of the two training runs this year, but he knows that that means absolutely nothing come race day. His opponents keep their cards close to their chest, never revealing too much about how they will do during the race. You don’t win during training; you win during the one hundred seconds when you are racing down the hill. And if there is one person who has mastered these one hundred seconds, it is Dream. Dream, who has that infuriating gift of watching the run of another athlete and finishing with two tenths of a second in his pocket—as if he could download other people’s racing lines, analysing their precise path through each turn for his benefit, mid-event.

George knows he’s got it in him to win here. He had been on the podium of the Birds of Prey four times—his very first podium in the World Cup had been on this slope. After Blade had retired, George had been so sure this would be his race and that the American dominance would finally be broken. Every single time he raced in Beaver Creek he had seen green when crossing the finish line—setting the fastest time of the day so far. Every single time, he cheered. Every single time, he had thought that this had to be it. And every single time, Blade and then later Dream had come after him and to show the celebrating home crowd that the Birds of Prey is their territory.

For once, the lottery gods have been kind to George. In yesterday’s bib lottery, he drew bib fifteen—racing as the last of the top skiers, just behind Dream. So at least he will know right away, whether he will beat Dream for once and he won’t have to wait for the idiot to finish the race and push him back to second place.

George’s shoulder gets caught uncomfortably on the next gate, making him lose balance momentarily. He curses and pushes Dream to the very back of his thoughts again, getting his head back in the game. Or rather the race, if you will.

Even when soaring almost fifty metres through the air, he doesn’t leave his crouched position, only straightening up slightly to prepare for the landing. Ignoring his burning thighs, he crouches back down again immediately. He should be reaching top speed right about now. 80 miles per hour down the hill—nothing will ever come as close to experience flying as this. That’s at least what George’s father always said when he was still skiing professionally.

George doesn’t remember much of what happened after the race was finished and the two of them were officially declared the joint winners. He was vaguely aware of the music, the flowers, and the champagne that was sprayed around and somehow ended up in his hair during the rushed podium celebration. The clicking of cameras. George had been smiling for the cameras, pretending he didn’t feel the press of Dream’s fingers like static even through the three layers of clothing between them.

Finally, they are funnelled into the mixed zone—a narrow maze of microphones and flashes—where exhausted athletes are meant to be charming in languages they barely speak.

“Georgie,” Larray says, a warning in his tone.

“No.”

Larray sighs deeply, as if he’s dealing with an idiot. “Listen,” he says. “I know you don’t go there much, but I have been online. I have seen the edits.”

“I’m going to throw up,” George says, staring at Larray in horror.

“They are,” Larray insists. “And you know what else? I don’t think the fans made that up from nothing. There is something there. That man is so into you.”

“He’s not,” George retorts, but he can’t deny how his heart flutters at the implication.

Larray points at him through the screen. If he weren't Larray, he might even look threatening. “He is.”

George shakes his head too fast. “You don’t know that.”

“I do,” Larray says. “Because he looks at you like you’re the only thing that matters. He doesn’t even look at his gold medals that way.”

George’s breath catches. Tears prick behind his eyes, hot and embarrassing. He blinks hard.

Larray softens immediately. “Hey,” he says. “You don’t have to do anything today. It’s Christmas. Go be weirdly glowy in your little snow cave.”

George swallows. “I’m not glowing.”

Larray smiles. “Sure.”

George wants to argue. Wants to insist that this is just the lighting, just the break, just the fact that, for once, his body isn’t running purely on pressure alone.

Dream’s hand on his waist in the elevator, steady and sure, unafraid of somebody stepping in. The way Dream had nearly looked… offended when George flirted back in that stuffy tent.

Maybe that’s what happiness is. Not the absence of the noise, but the presence of someone who turns it down enough to be bearable.

George swallows, because it’s terrifying to admit that out loud. 

He whispers, barely audible, “I might… talk to him.”

Larray’s grin turns victorious. “That’s my baby boy.”

Halfway through, he catches himself thinking about how Dream would make fun of him for this and then—worse—how Dream would probably slide his arm under his neck and squeeze him close whenever a penguin died.

The new year arrives and, with it, Kitzbühel—the infamous Downhill race in Austria.

George tells himself he will talk to Dream when the race is over, to keep both of their minds focused on the sport, just as their rules say.

His resolve lasts exactly three hours, which is how long it takes for Dream to catch him after training, with that same stupid knitted beanie and the exact kind of gentle, careful attention that makes George want to both run away and into his arms.

“Hey,” Dream says with a smile, absolutely unaware that he has been living rent-free in George’s head.

George’s mouth goes dry. “Hey.”

The words he rehearsed all week line up behind his teeth and refuse to move. 

Dream’s expression shifts—something watchful underneath the joking. “You okay?” he asks, quieter.

George should say: I need to talk to you.

Instead, he says, “Your room.”

Dream’s eyes widen slightly, caught off guard by George’s suddenness. Then he nods once. “Yeah,” he says. “Sure.”

Inside his room, Dream kisses him as if he’s been just as desperate for them to meet again as George has been.

It doesn’t even take ten seconds for George’s plan to dissolve into simmering desire.

They end up on the bed with their clothes half-off and hands everywhere, and Dream keeps checking in with him in the small ways that have become so familiar in the few weeks they have been doing this. “This okay?” murmured with his mouth pressed against George’s neck. “Tell me if you need me to stop,” said when he’s pushing in, as if George could do anything but encourage him to keep going.

Teeth gently scrape against sensitive skin before Dream’s lips start sucking. George is sure he will leave a mark, but he doesn’t find a single cell within him that minds. On the contrary, he feels an almost ridiculous spark of triumph. Love bite, his brain decides this time, hopeful and humiliating and impossible to take back. After, he tells himself. He will talk with him after.

After, when George is warm and loose-limbed and stupidly happy inside, he lies on Dream’s chest and tries to find the courage again.

Dream’s heartbeat knocks gently against his ear, his hand rubbing soothing circles into George’s back. The outside world feels far away—sealed behind walls and curtains and snow—and for a moment in time, the world reduces to only the two of them.

He should talk about the thing that is doing funny things to his heart, but he finds that he doesn’t know how. So he reaches for something adjacent. An in. Something safe, something that will tell Dream I see you. I want you. I love you.

Dream’s chest rises with an inhale under him. “Of course.”

“You’re… very adamant about the whole consent thing.” He rushes on, not wanting this to come out the wrong way. “I’m not complaining. I like it. I just—noticed. And I am wondering where it comes from.”

Dream’s answer isn’t immediate. The silence is short, but it shifts the atmosphere around them. When he speaks, his voice is more guarded than usual. “Because, when I was younger, nobody asked me for a yes.”

George’s throat tightens. When he asked the question, he wasn’t sure what he was expecting. Whatever it was, it wasn’t this. He lifts his head slightly, trying to read Dream’s face.

Dream keeps staring at the ceiling. “My first team only had one gear,” he says. “Push. Push. Push. If you said you were tired, they told you good—push through. If you were sore, they sent you to physio and told you to go again. If you skied well, they said again. If you crashed…” He swallows. “They showed you the footage, pointed out your mistake and said again.”

Something inside of George goes quiet in a way that scares him. His fingers gently stroke over Dream’s chest, encouraging him to keep going.

“It sounds normal until you realize there’s no place for no,” Dream says quietly. He breathes out a laugh that isn’t a laugh at all. “I was sixteen and already thinking about never skiing again.”

George’s chest aches. “Dream—”

“My parents noticed it,” Dream continues, voice low. “They pulled me out, and we switched everything. It felt stupid at the time, like I was the weak one. But it saved me.”

George lies there, stunned by how much damage can hide beneath medals and smiles.

“And now you…?” George asks softly, afraid to break the vulnerability surrounding them.

“Now I ask,” Dream says, simple as that. “I ask on the hill. I ask in bed. I ask myself.” His mouth turns into a small, self-deprecating smile. “You don’t win long-term if you only know how to get through the next race.” 

Then, Dream presses a kiss to George’s curls. 

“I like you,” he says, warm and certain. “So I will never push past the point where it stops being good.”

The words I like you should feel good. They do, briefly. A soft flare underneath George’s ribs. And then he latches onto something sharp and cruel: that’s all it is. Like. Not love. Not the word he wanted to hear. George swallows the sting down fast because this is, objectively, ridiculous. It’s just a word. People use it all the time. There is no need to crash out over a technicality.

Dream shifts slightly beneath him, careful not to jostle George off of him, then says—almost too casually, “I wear my heart on my sleeve, George. It’s gotten me taken advantage of before.” His mouth quirks like it’s a joke, but his eyes don’t match it. “So… I try to be careful. Ask. Check.”

George goes cold in a slow, sick way. Because if Dream wears his heart on his sleeve—if Dream is the kind of person who says what he feels—then Dream would have said it, wouldn’t he?

If Dream wanted more, George thinks he’d know. Dream doesn’t hide the things that matter. He doesn’t hide his appreciation for the staff, his love for his teammates. From all George knows about him, Dream doesn’t do subtle. 

George has seen Dream want things. Dream wants loudly—with his whole face, with his whole body, with no shame in it. He flirts with his friends, praises his team. Dream kisses George like he’s starving, and then smiles like it’s the easiest thing in the world to let him go.

And George—stupidly, embarrassingly—has been treating the way Dream touches him as a sign that Dream feels the same. As if the way Dream says his name means more than it does. He’s been so desperate to be seen as anything other than just a result that he has mistaken attention for love. 

Larray was wrong too, George realises. Larray saw what he wanted to see—his best friend finally soft, finally happy, finally touched by something good.

And George saw what he wanted to see. If George went looking, really looking, he’d probably find hundreds of clips of Dream grinning at Sam the exact same way. Probably find interviews where Dream’s eyes soften for someone else and George just never noticed, because he was too busy falling in love with him.

The hope in George’s chest goes very, very still. 

And just like that, he swallows everything he meant to say and tucks it away to a place where it can’t catch up with him.

Dream’s fingers gently comb through George’s hair, bringing him back to the moment. He watches George’s face like he’s waiting for George to say something.

And George came here with one thing in his mind. To tell Dream how he feels. But now he can see the trapdoor underneath the confession, barely hidden by kind words and gentle touches.

Because if George says it, Dream will have to do something with that. Dream will have to answer. And while George can handle losing his medals to Dream, he cannot survive losing his heart to him, too.

So Dream waits, and George says nothing, and Dream—mercifully—doesn’t push.

Of course, he doesn’t. Dream never pushes. Dream never takes. And that’s exactly why George can’t afford to want more of him—because Dream would be gentle, even when he breaks him.

George shifts a little, a restless charge starting to hum through his bones, his body trying to outrun the feeling crawling up his throat. Suddenly, he is too aware of how close he is to saying something he can’t take back. 

I want you. I want more. Please want me back.

The sheet is twisted too tightly around his waist. The pressure of it feels unbearable, making him feel trapped in a situation he can’t escape. He tugs it loose, trying to breathe, and immediately hates how exposed it makes him feel.

Dream’s eyes track the movement, and before George can finally figure out what to say or how to get them back on track, Dream leans down to grab George’s briefs and presses them into his hand with a small smile that’s meant to make it easier.

There it is. The polite exit.

And just like that, they are back to their routine. A routine they’ve built so carefully, brick by brick, as if it’s not a bruise forming in George’s throat.

He swings his legs off the bed. The carpet is cold under his feet.

George gets dressed. Puts his armour back on piece by piece, until he can breathe again. Underwear first. Shirt. Pants. He pulls the fabric up, smooths it down, fixes the waistband with fingers that don’t quite feel like his.

Zipper. Button. Belt.

He finds his socks underneath the small couch, rolls them over his feet, tugging at them until the seams sit right. Shoes. One lace, then the other. Tighten. Double-knot. Again, for the second shoe.

Jacket. Phone. The keycard to his hotel.

At the door, Dream kisses him goodbye as usual. A quiet good night is whispered against his lips.

George manages to nod. “Night.”

Rule one, he reminds himself, no staying over.

George doesn’t cry on the walk back to his hotel. He doesn’t cry when he showers off the proof of their night together, even when the hot water hits the tender mark on his neck and makes it sting.

In the mirror, it looks darker than it felt. Too real. Too earned for something that isn’t his.

He presses his thumb to it once. Hickey, his brain supplies. A stupid little hiccup in his life.

Morning comes with no warmth.

He drinks half a bottle of water in three gulps, trying to swish the taste of morning out of his mouth. Then, he moves through his routine on autopilot—stretch, work out the tightness in his hips, tape what needs taping. Small, controllable things. Things that have a clear order. Things that don’t ask him how he feels.

It’s only a training run today. A chance to put his body back in its lane before tomorrow’s race asks for everything.

He leaves the room without looking back.

He’s halfway through a set of squats when the air shifts. George doesn’t have to look up to know Dream is there. Dream has a way of pulling attention like gravity, and George has gotten used to orbiting him against his will.

“Hey,” George says, not knowing what else to say.

Dream’s expression flickers. It’s so small, George almost misses it. Dream’s gaze drops to George’s neck, then snaps back up like he didn’t mean to.

“You good?” Dream asks, brows furrowed.

George can feel the question settle between them. You good. You okay. Dream always asks. Dream always checks. Dream always—

“I’m fine,” he says, because the shorter he keeps his answer, the smaller the chance he breaks apart.

“Okay,” Dream says. He doesn’t sound offended or angry at George not giving him anything. He doesn’t sound much like anything at all. It somehow makes George feel worse. “Have a good run.”

And then he turns away and walks off, seemingly easy and unbothered.

Of course, he thinks, sour and sharp. Of course, it’s that easy for you. Just as everything else.

He knows how hard Dream trains—has seen the results of it firsthand, felt them beneath his fingertips, traced each muscle one by one. He knows he shouldn’t diminish Dream’s effort, not even in the privacy of his own mind. He doesn’t want to.

Two.

One.

He could tell them that the day had felt wrong long before he ever clipped into his skis. That he had noticed it in the quiet, grey hour before the alarm went off—lying awake in the narrow hotel bed while the radiator clicked softly and the mountains outside the window were still nothing but black shapes against the sky.

Fractions of seconds.

Fractions of centimetres.

Fractions of attention.

“No,” he lies, and doesn’t think about it more. He doesn’t want to ruin his chances to sound even remotely believable. 

The reporter’s eyebrows lift ever so slightly, anyway. 

“So you don’t feel any added pressure?” She digs deeper, clearly hoping for a better headline. “From the comparisons? The speculation?”

“Once you’re in the start gate, it’s just you and the course,” he says.

“But what about Dream specifically? He’s not just any other athlete after all,” she tries again, not giving up just yet. “Does skiing against the best skier of the present change anything for you?”

He kind of wants to laugh, but at least he has been part of this circus long enough to know that laughing would make headlines for all the wrong reasons. 

“We’re competitors,” he says. “We all push each other. That’s—,” he clears his throat, “—that’s the point. I ski better because of him.” He nods once, polite and final, and steps back. “Thanks.”

Before he leaves, he shoots a final glance at the screen, where he sees Dream take the leader’s chair.
