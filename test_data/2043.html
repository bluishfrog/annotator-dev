Summary

Nineteen years ago, Macklin Celebrini was forced into the NHL's top secret omega breeding program. Eighteen years ago, his pup was stolen right from his chest in the hospital room after twenty hours of labor, leaving a devastated omega and his equally devastated alpha, Will Smith. For years, the top line pair have fought to find their pup with no luck. Now, on the cusp of his final season, Mack just can't do it anymore. He's ready to hang his skates and retire after this year.

Until a new scent shows up in the locker room on the first day of Training Camp. A familiar scent.

Mack drives into training camp even more exhausted than last year. He pounds back the latte Will made him, sipping the dregs before setting the cup neatly back in the cup holder. There’s always something better about coffee made by Will. It’s just always tasted better. Will refuses to tell him the secret, but Mack stopped asking a long time ago.

He’s the first to the rink. That’s not unusual. It’s only unusual that Will isn’t here beside him, ready to complain about how young the new rookies look. He only says it to Mack and the other older vets now, under his breath and huffy. But once he’s inside, Will always lands right in the middle of the group—the first to offer to play sewer ball or ask a new guy about his family. If Mack is the C, then Will is every inch the A. Not for the first time, Mack wonders how he’s supposed to do this without Will by his side.

The surgery went well over the off season, but recovery has been a real bitch. Will’s knees aren’t what they used to be, but he still has another few years left in him. He can thank the alpha in him for that, for better genes that bounce back easier. 

Mack already knows this is his last year. He can’t keep doing this. His body is broken down, his soul held together by Will’s half-assed sewing. If it weren’t for Will…

He slams the car door and then has to open it again to get the stupid empty coffee cup out to throw away. Usually, Will would handle that for both of them. He’d be singing the last song from their ride in and gathering all the trash to throw out. He hates a messy car.

Inside, everything looks the exact same as it did last year. The place has been overhauled in the last decade, sure, but it’s not unrecognizable from the same arena he called home when he was eighteen. Mack walks past several banners of his own face, action shots of him and Will winning the Western Conference final seven years ago. To this day, it’s the closest they’ve gotten to the Stanley Cup. A slew of injuries had caught up to them. Losing both goalies in back to back games just wasn’t fair. With no competent goalies, there could be no Cup. Getting to the final and losing remains one of Mack’s biggest regrets.

His second biggest regret.

He’s the first on the ice. No matter how much he trains over the summer, there’s nothing like cutting that first line out onto fresh ice the first day of training camp. There’s something magical about it, about a fresh start. He’s always liked training camp. He’s always liked getting an idea of who’s going to work well together, on where he and Will fit into the bigger picture of the team, getting a feel for the team chemistry.

They could do it this year, he thinks insanely. They could win the Cup. Will should be back in a month. They just scored the first overall in the draft a few months past, some wunderkind named Philip Johnson. As the captain, Mack had reached out, congratulated him on joining the team, the same platitudes that hockey players have passed back and forth for ages. But the kid was polite and enthusiastic, and something about him makes Mack nostalgic. 

He remembers when he was that young, that naive. He sees so much of himself in Philip already—both first overalls, both omegas…

Mack pushes over the bucket of pucks and takes off with one towards the goal. He won’t let what happened to him happen to Philip. Never again. He won’t let them—

This league isn’t kind to omegas.

His body feels good. He takes extra time to stretch his muscles out as the boys start filtering into the locker room. Mack makes time for all of them, the familiar scents of the team under his nose. He’s missed them. There are new threads to the team scent, a lack of Will that Mack brings etched into his own skin. Some of the guys here will be sent down to the Cuda, and some will stay up and become brothers in arms.

New faces greet him. It’s wild to be the Sidney Crosby of his generation. He’s now that grizzled vet with the good reputation and the list of accolades to his name. He’s the one putting on the C for Canada. He’s the one these young rookies look up to, who tells stories about talking to him to impress their friends. He’d find it amusing, but he’s just—he’s so tired.

Normally, Will would be working the room for him. Mack misses him like a limb, though he’s just at their house in bed. Will was more upset about missing camp than Mack was, but it was close. After so many years, he feels bereft without him.

And then, out of nowhere, there’s a new scent that pricks at Mack’s brain. He breathes in deeply, not something one usually wants to do in a locker room. It’s there, just—just out of reach.

Mack looks around desperately, trying to find the source of that smell. It’s—it’s important.

This is a Before. He knows that he’s sitting on the cusp of something really life-changing, and there’s nothing he can do to change it.

His head swivels around. Decker and Lionel are punching each other in front of Baeder’s stall while Prinny watches in amusement. Mack knows their scents and discounts them, moving on to where Brownie and Hazer are talking to a freshly traded defenseman. Not them either.

Where is it coming from? 

Mack stands up, every nerve in his body on edge like he’ll need to outrun a volcano or something. The scent is getting stronger, despite him being frozen on the spot. It’s something flowery, softer than Will’s rose scent, but it’s also something else that Mack can’t name. 

He looks to the left where Jesper is getting into his goalie pads, and when he turns back around to keep looking for the scent, there’s a body standing in front of him. That body is carrying the scent that Mack has been searching for. A few minutes, eighteen years, his whole life…

“Hi,” the voice says, too eager and high pitched. Brown hair and green eyes meet him head on. There’s something about his smile, like he’s got a secret that only he knows, but a good one. It’s Will’s smile.

“Philip,” Mack says, breathless. He’s going to have a heart attack right here in the locker room if he’s not careful. “Philip Johnson.”

“Yeah, that’s me,” he says and holds out a fist for Mack to bump. He notes that his hand is shaking—both of their hands are shaking, albeit for wildly different reasons. Mack doesn’t leave him hanging. He never will.

“Good to—good to meet you,” Mack manages to say. It’s more true than anything else he’s ever said.

“It’s so cool to meet you, Mr. Celebrini,” the boy says, all enthusiasm and puppy energy.

“Just—not that,” Mack says, trying to keep himself composed. “You don’t have to—just Mack, please.” He doesn’t offer up his hockey nickname. It would be weird to hear Celly out of that mouth.

“Mack,” Philip repeats to himself, like Mack’s just handed him the best prize.

Mack’s going to vomit all over. He feels sick down to his soul. Sick and terrified and like his life’s achievements are all right there at once—everything he’s ever wanted. No, everything he’s ever needed.

Fuck, he wishes Will was here. Will would know what to do.

Somehow, Mack finds himself on the ice with everyone else. By some miracle, Mack goes through enough motions that no one calls him out on how he’s acting—standoffish and in his own head. They must put it down to Will being out. The vets likely will. Everyone else doesn’t know him well enough to say anything.

Philip Johnson. Philip Johnson. Philip. Philip wasn’t on the list.

He runs through drills with precision, slower than even five years ago, but with the dogged determination he’s known for. In his head, though, he spins everything around.

He needs to tell Will. He doesn’t know how to—

Will is better at coming up with plans. Will will know how to handle it, how to—

The kid is good, too. Mack watches him fly over the ice, graceful and grinning like he was born to be there. And, well, he was, wasn’t he? The thought makes Mack sick. He skates to the bench to down some water, clear out his dry mouth. He wishes Will was here. A few of the trainers ask about him, and Mack speaks for both himself and Will. They’re the club’s worst kept secret. 

He’s watching the ice as Philip sinks one past Jesper, failing to hide his excitement. He’s like a puppy, and suddenly Mack wants to fall through the ice and never get back up.

The locker room after the first day of camp is full of chatter and excitement. These are the prospects eager to make the roster, hopeful to have instant chemistry with the team and fall into a slot. 

Mack sits at his stall, the empty one to his right a black hole where Will usually sits. If only that was the only thing dragging his thoughts into a spiral. He keeps himself together. He has to. He’s learned when it’s okay to let it all go, and he’s not there yet. Part of him wants to call his dad, but he outgrew that instinct so many years ago that he can’t give into it now.

He showers off quickly, one of the first. Guilt nags at him, making him realize he needs to get some words in with each of these guys. Some of them will be team in a few weeks when the season starts. He knows what it means when the older guys—when the super stars lower themselves back into being human.

As he’s whipping his hair back and forth to get the rest of the moisture out while Decker chats at him about his summer, a strange man enters the locker room behind Coach Pims.

“Johnson,” Pims calls out, waving an arm in summons. 

Philip’s head bobs up in askance. “Coach?”

“With me,” Pims barks, “someone here to meet with you.”

Mack looks at the man behind Coach, not recognizing him. A horrible feeling settles in his guts. Mack knows every single person in the Sharks organization, if not by name, then by face. This includes all the workers who would have access back into their locker room. He reluctantly knows all the beat reporters that cover San Jose and many of the ones that don’t.

He doesn’t recognize this guy.

It’s time to step up. He’s the captain here.

“Who’s this?” Mack asks, walking quickly and trying not to look like he is.

“Oh, Mack,” Pims says, astonished. Mack’s not usually one to insert himself into a situation. “It’s nothing. Just the omega rights org wanting to speak with our young, new hotshot omega. No need to—”

Mack’s hackles rise. The horrible feeling intensifies. He can feel Philip at his back, confused at the situation and at Mack’s behavior—he’s ignored him pretty much since their introduction this morning.

Mack turns to stare at the man behind Coach. Thick neck, older. He’s a beta, and he smells sour, or at least he smells sour to Mack.

“Philip has the right to have his agent or parent present when speaking with your organization,” Mack says, reciting the policies that he helped Sidney Crosby write into the bylines. He has them memorized. “In the absence of a parent or agent, another figure of authority in his life can advocate on his behalf.”

The locker room has fallen silent around them.

“Philip,” Mack says, not taking his eyes off the omega rights rep. “Do you have an agent or parent here?”

There’s a small hesitation, but then his clear voice says, “No, I don’t.”

Mack’s heart drops into his stomach. “Then a captain will do,” Mack says, nodding at the rep. “Show us the way.”

The man’s eyes narrow, but he nods.

Coach looks confused, glancing between Mack and Philip with curiosity. He wasn’t here nineteen years ago. He wasn’t here when Mack had this same conversation. Sure, it was a different rep, a different man, but it seems the tactics haven’t changed no matter how much reform Mack and Sid put into everything.

Down the hallway, towards Coach’s office, Mack walks behind Coach but slightly ahead of Philip. Under his breath, he says, “Do not under any circumstances be alone with anyone from the omega rights office.”

Philip’s eyes are huge with concern and confusion, but he nods. 

“Trust me, Philip,” Mack says, letting a hint of how the last nineteen years have haunted him into his voice. 

“You can use this room here,” Pims says, eyeing them warily. “I’ll just be next door in my office.” He doesn’t look away as the rep enters the conference room they usually use for small player meetings. 

Mack allows Philip to sit in the chair across the desk from the rep while he hangs back beside the door, leaning propped up against the wall with his arms over his chest. He wonders how this guy is going to play it, if he’ll put up pretenses or just go straight for the jugular. Mack will wait; he wants to give him the rope to swing on. 

“So, Philip,” the rep says, his face morphing into something resembling a pleasant expression. It’s eerie. “I’m here to introduce myself as your omega rights representative for the Sharks. My name is Steve. You’ll be seeing a lot of me.”

Philip shuffles in his chair. Mack can practically feel the confusion from here. 

“Hi,” Philip says when it’s clear no one else is going to say anything. “What exactly do you… do?”

Mack almost snorts at the question. Good. Philip’s picked up on how weird this is.

“I’ll be in charge of the optics,” Steve says smoothly. 

“The optics of what?” Philip asks, turning around in his chair so that he can glance at Mack in confusion. 

“Yeah, Steve,” Mack says, unable to help himself. “The optics of what?”

Steve ignores them, continuing as if neither of them had spoken. “When omegas are allowed to join—” Steve pauses, shoots a half second look at Mack, and changes course. “Omegas were not always welcome in the NHL. That’s a well documented fact. Over the course of the last few decades, we’ve managed to find a balance between the alpha, beta, and omega players that allows for omegas to be an integral part of the team. They provide talents that were, let’s say, lacking before.”

Omegas were only allowed into the league because Sidney Fucking Crosby was so good at hockey, and so many teams were salivating after him, that they changed the whole goddamn rule book. But it wasn’t enough that he dominated, won three cups, won every prize there is to win. No, that wasn’t enough. He was still an omega, and the league needed to make sure he knew his place. 

“You mean, like, how Mack is the captain and keeps everybody calm under pressure? Or, like, how often Mack has been the scoring leader on this team because people constantly underestimate him?” Philip asks with another look back at Mack.

Meanwhile, Mack’s heart is dropping into his stomach. They’re going to ask him already? They’re going to push this agenda when he’s barely gotten into the locker room?

Not on Mack’s fucking life.

“Mr. Celebrini earned his place on the team,” Steve says carefully. “He followed the path forward and paid his dues.”

“And it’s the greatest regret of my life,” Mack says, keeping his voice as casually as he can. “The rep back then did the same thing to me, preyed on my naiveté and innocence. He made me think that I couldn’t be on the team without going through the program.” Mack looks at Philip, begging him to understand. “I’m here to tell you that’s fucking bullshit. Your place is already earned on this team, Philip. You don’t owe us anything.”

“Owe what?” Philip asks, looking between Mack and Steve. “What program? What are you asking me to do?”

“The breeding program,” Mack says, choking on the word. There’s no official program, of course not. Googling that term and the NHL will bring up nothing, less than nothing. The league pays people a lot of money to make sure it doesn’t come up. There are no rumors on Reddit. There are no gossip pages. There’s nothing but the scars of the omegas who pay the price to play in the league. The contracts are buried so deep in Bettman’s office that they’ll never see the light of day.

But they exist.

“The omega program allows the franchise the option for more talent in the future,” Steve explains with a cutting look to Mack like he’s daring him to disagree. “It’s a few months of being careful, and then our participants are free to live the rest of their lives however they want.”

Mack snorts again. “What a way of explaining that you want to use his womb for potential players in the future.”

That’s the other part they don’t share in this initial meeting—the omega signs up to have a pup, but the franchise never plans for them to actually raise that baby. No, that would be too much, splitting their attention from the standings. Instead, they’ll give the baby to a willing couple to raise, check in on them, force the boys to play hockey to see if they have any talent, and when they show promise, they’ll rig the lottery with Bettman to make sure their contracted pups come back to the franchise who created them. That’s how they see it, anyway. It’s a transaction, a business deal, another tool in their arsenal to trade for players and prospects. They created the product, and when it’s ripe, they demand a return on their investment. 

The parents of that child don’t need to be told anything about their pup, not even the ones who want the baby. They string the omega parent along with just enough intel that they can keep them under management’s thumb just for a modicum of news. It’s insanely effective.

Sometimes the franchise even gets to pick the alpha for maximum talent chance. The only saving grace for Mack was that he got to choose Smitty, that they didn’t look for an outside alpha to expand the gene pool. He got to choose Smitty, and he’s never regretted that except that he brought just as much pain to Will as to himself. 

The breeding program is a disgusting practice. Mack has never been more proud of anything than the work he and Sid and Will have done in the shadows to cut down on this. He’s honestly shocked the Sharks would try it with their first new omega in nineteen years. 

“You steal babies?” Philip asks, unknowingly mimicking the distaste in Mack’s churning stomach.

“We don’t steal babies. We only work with willing omegas,” Steve says through gritted teeth. He’s really regretting letting Mack into this meeting.

“’Willing’ makes it sound like you’re going to give him a choice. We both know that’s not true. That’s not the play,” Mack says, pushing off the wall to stand behind Philip and place a hand on his chair.

“Of course he has a choice,” Steve says.

“So, you weren’t going to intimidate him and make him think he has to agree to this or he won’t be offered a spot on the roster?” Mack asks, knowing these tactics from personal experience and from others. These bullies love to play the card that they won’t let the omegas play hockey. Knowing what he knows now, there was never a chance they were going to leave their superstar off the team, not with so much buzz around his name. “He has a spot on the roster even without going through with the program. He’ll probably play even better if he’s not pregnant, you know.”

Steve grunts and stands up, placing his hands on the table. “You don’t know what you’re talking about Celebrini.”

“Oh, so things have changed so much since I was eighteen and sitting in this very spot?” Mack looks down at Philip. 

“Of course things have changed in the last eighteen years,” Steve says. His face is turning red. Good. Mack hopes he chokes on his own spit and fucking dies.

“He’ll have the option to raise his own pup?” Mack asks, lifting his eyebrows in challenge. “He gets to choose his alpha?”

“I…I don’t…I wouldn’t say…” Steve sputters. “He doesn’t need the split responsibility of—”

“So, you’re still in the business of stealing babies,” Mack concludes, letting the full disgust he’s been carrying around for years out.

“A signed contract between both parties consenting to—”

“I never consented to having my pup taken from me,” Mack argues. “I was mislead and purposefully isolated so neither my parents nor my agent could intervene on my behalf. The program made me have to lie to the world, to my family, and still have to give up my baby in the end. He was stolen off my chest when I was too weak to fight back, you prick.”

“What the fuck is going on?” Philip asks, panicky. His scent is growing sharper—distressed omega making everyone’s tempers flare even harder.

“Philip Johnson respectfully declines the program,” Mack says with all the authority he can beg, borrow, or steal. “And he won’t be penalized for not participating. If you have a problem with this, you’ll take it up with me.”

“For someone who claims to hate this program so much, you sure stuck around,” Steve says, like he has to say something, like he won’t let anyone else get the last word. “I guess it didn’t matter that much to you in the end. You’re still here, aren’t you?”

Mack smirks. “I stayed here for one reason, Steve. I stayed to find the pup that was stolen from me, knowing the Sharks had first dibs at him. I have only ever stayed here for that reason.”

Steve flashes an ugly smile like he thinks he’s won. “Then, you’ll do what you’re supposed to do. You’ll stay here, and you’ll play until you can’t anymore. Looks like the program won in the end, didn’t it?”

Mack laughs, a wild laugh that he’s never heard from himself before. “No, actually,” he says, “this will be my last season.”

“And why’s that? Giving up already?”

Already? It’s been eighteen years since his baby was taken from him. Nineteen since he fell pregnant.

“No, I just finally found what I’ve been looking for,” Mack says and then lets himself feel the joy. He pushes down the anger, the years of bitterness, the anxiety, the grief, the mourning that has been hounding him since he was a kid himself. “You’re not going to do to my pup what you did to me. Over my dead fucking body. C’mon Philip.”

Mack storms out of the room, taking care that Philip is trailing behind him. He holds the door open and shoves the kid out before him, determined to keep himself in between his pup and the threat. Then he takes off at a fast clip to put as much space between them and that office as possible.

“What the fuck?” Philip says, but he’s following Mack at a close heel. “No, really what the fuck?”

Mack leads them through a few hallways down one with a larger equipment room that he doesn’t think sees a lot of traffic. “Two minutes.”

The rest of the team have left, but the equipment managers haven’t had time to clean up behind them. The place is a sty, and Mack pinches his nose. He doesn’t want to smell anything other than his pup, the pup he’s been waiting eighteen years to see and hold and love.

Mack finds the room, opens the door and allows Philip to go in first.

When the door closes behind him, leaving the world outside this bubble, it catches up to him. Suddenly, he’s breathing deep gulps of air, unable to get the oxygen low enough into his lungs. He bends over, hands on his knees, replaying the entire conversation and how it could have gone.

Shit. Shit shit shit shit. 

“Mack, are you…?” Philip looks just as panicked, his matching green eyes wide and scared. His scent is all over the place, terrified and anxious, but always the same undertones as Will’s.

Mack holds up a finger, his breath wheezing out of him. He finds his phone and hits dial. 

A familiar voice answers the phone and doesn’t stop for a greeting. Why would he? They never bother to greet each other. “Mack? Are you stopping to get food on your way home because I was hoping we could do—”

“Will—” he manages to get out.

His partner—his beautiful partner who he hasn’t been able to mate yet because then Mack wouldn’t be able to play in the NHL—stops. “Mack, are you okay? Are you hurt?”

“Will—” he repeats, trying to get it out, trying to let the joy out. He needs to tell him. He needs to be the first to know, after Philip. He tries a few more times, but nothing comes out. 

“Here, let me,” Philip says, taking the phone from his hand. His son—his son!—gives him a strange look, like he doesn’t know how to handle him. And he doesn’t, does he? He never got the opportunity to learn him. “Um, Mr.—” Philip looks down at the contact name. Thank God Mack left it as Will Smith (Emerg Contact). “Mr. Smith, he’s—Mack’s—um…”

Wow, so Mack’s son is just as good at this as he is. Will must start speaking, giving instructions, probably, because Philip listens. He waits a beat and then he finds a bucket to turn over so Mack can sit down. 

“He’s on his way,” Philip says unnecessarily. Of course he’s on his way. No matter what state his knees are in, Mack’s alpha won’t be able to stand hearing him like this. He would have been on his way the second he registered Mack’s voice. “He says to keep you breathing, and—and that he’ll make it better when he’s here.”

Mack nods. Just looking at Philip calms him some. His breaths ease. The pressure in his chest starts to let up. They stare at each other, green eyes to green eyes. What else can Mack find in him that came from his parents? What is his alone? 

“Are you—was that—” Philip gulps and then raises his hands and puts them on his head. “What the fuck?”

He can’t answer. Philip doesn’t seem to really need or want an answer—he’s not pressing. They sit in a stilted silence, staring at each other every once in a while. Mack has no idea how to play this. He’s been thinking of this moment for years and years, dreaming of it. Some nights, imagining himself finding his pup and speaking to him was the only thing that got him through the night and into the morning.

Somehow, he didn’t think it would be this hard. He never accounted for the reality of it, that this is a stranger who doesn’t know him. This is a boy who doesn’t even seem to know he was stolen from his parents, his parents that loved him and wanted him and prepared for him.

“You had a great practice,” Mack says when words come back to him. He rests his head in his hands and keeps taking deep breaths. “Good hands. You’re going to do great things here.”

“Mack.” Philip says his name in the same tone Will does. “Mack, seriously, what the fuck? What was that back there? Why’d you lie about…”

Mack’s head shoots up again. “Lie?”

“My parents are in Winnipeg,” he says, more confused than ever. “I’m not—they’re my parents.”

Will walks in just then. Neither Mack nor Philip told him the closet they’d be in, but he knew. He always knows. And fuck, he must have broken several traffic laws to get here so quickly. Or Mack was out of it for longer than he realized, but probably both.

“Mack,” Will says, hobbling over and pulling him into his arms. “You okay now?”

“Couldn’t breathe, but yeah I’m okay now,” Mack says into Will’s neck. His alpha scent works wonders to calm him down. Roses and fire and something else. 

“You scared me. You couldn’t talk, and then—you haven’t been like that in a long time.” He’s talking about the years after their rookie season when Mack couldn’t sleep, couldn’t eat, could barely play without embarrassing himself. He was more panic attack than he was player. 

When he wasn’t in a fit of depression, he was frantic with energy trying to pursue every avenue to find his baby—contacting private investigators, following Sharks front office workers home to see if they stole his child, rifling through important looking papers in the GM’s office while Smitty played look out. All to no avail. They hid him deep in fucking Winnipeg apparently.

“And then some rookie is talking to me,” Will goes on, rubbing his hand along Mack’s back to soothe both of them. “No offense, kid,” he adds to Philip. 

“None… taken?” Philip says, staring at the two of them.

“What’s wrong? What happened?” Will asks, voice steady in the way it goes when he’s gearing up to handle something. It’s the tone of voice that he’s about to burn the world down if it’ll help Mack.

“Omega rights org showed up,” Mack says, feeling Will freeze in his embrace.

“What did those fuckers want with you now?” he asks, pure venom shooting out of him. He doesn’t even give Mack a chance to answer. “They don’t want you to… again, do they? Have they lost their minds? Once wasn’t enough? Is this punishment for getting older and slower?”

Even with all their work, Sid and Mack couldn’t get the breeding program entirely eliminated, but they got most teams to agree to waiting to ask until their omegas were older, after their prime. Mack never asked Sid why that was important to him, but he can guess. They have a silent understanding to not talk about why either of them are so passionate about shutting this shit down.

“I’m not slower,” Mack protests, choosing the stupidest thing to respond to. “It’s not—not for me.” He looks over his shoulder at Philip. “For the other omega on the team, Philip.”

Will whips around. “You’re not going to do the breeding program, are you? You better not have signed anything, kid. They talk shit, but there are provisions in the NHLPA that keep this from happening. We made sure of it. They can only ask you now. They aren’t supposed to ask kids brand new to the league. It’s supposed to only be aging omegas who want to give back, not—this is bullshit.”

“Mack wouldn’t let them even ask,” Philip says, looking strangely between the two of them like he knows he’s not supposed to be witnessing this behavior. They’re an open secret on the team, but Will and Mack are still a secret. 

“Damn right, baby,” Will says, curling his hand around the back of Mack’s neck. He wouldn’t normally treat Mack so obviously like his omega, not at the rink, but this is a special circumstance.

“Smell him,” Mack tells Will, pointing his nose at Philip. “Smell him, Smitty.”

“I know you’re constantly paranoid that I’m going to up and leave you for another omega—which is crazy, by the way—but I’m certainly not going to leave you for a child who plays on our team, probably our line. Are you out of your—”

“Smitty, smell him,” Mack says again, more insistent. Yeah, he’s had some weird hangups in the past due to the trauma of how everything shook out, about his alpha rejecting him because he lost their baby, things he couldn’t control in the aftermath of everything, but this isn’t that. He needs Will to get it. He needs Will to see what he sees, to smell what he knows to be true.

Will rolls his eyes and takes a step back from Mack like such close proximity would throw off the scent. He’s not wrong. Mack watches his nose twitch, remembering with fondness how he used to breathe before they finally fixed his septum, so he sees the exact moment Will understands.

“What the fuck,” Will says, not a question but a statement. His nostrils flare as he steps closer to Philip.

“I know,” Mack says with fireworks in his chest. “Can you believe it?”

“Mack,” Will says, close to tears. Mack hasn’t seen him cry in years, not since they lost the Stanley Cup.

“I know!” Mack says. “I know. I almost shit myself.”

“What are you guys talking about?” Philip says, sounding small. His arms are wrapped around his chest.

“You smell like us,” Will says simply.

It’s not an accusation, just an observation. It’s factual. Philip Johnson, first overall and generational talent, smells like Smitty and Mack. He looks like Mack, but he has Will’s smile. He’s a blend of them and something unique of his own. His scent isn’t definitive. None of this is air tight evidence, but add all these elements up, and it’s no surprise that both Mack and Will have come to the same conclusion.

“I grew up in Winnipeg!” Philip bursts out. “With my parents! And my siblings and—families can smell different. Everybody knows that. You guys are on crack.”

“He was in Winnipeg?” Will says to Mack, a scrunch of his face echoing Mack’s thoughts.

Mack knows they need to handle this delicately. Philip is already freaking out. He doesn’t want to add to his stress. He shoots Will a look to follow his lead, and Will nods. That’s one thing about them—they take turns leading. Will’s a big enough alpha to know that sometimes Mack’s way is better.

“I’m not trying to freak you out, Philip,” he says, standing to his full height. “Obviously, I don’t have any proof, but the facts are like this: your birthday is in August. You don’t smell like your parents or your siblings. You were encouraged to play hockey from a young age, maybe you won some bullshit scholarships or something. The people who raised you prioritized your hockey, even if you wanted to quit, they wouldn’t let you. You’ve always known in your gut that something was off, but you didn’t know what.”

Philip takes a step backwards. “How did you…?”

“You smell like us,” Mack says because it bears repeating. “You have my eyes. You have the smile of your alpha father. You play like him, too. I noticed that earlier today. I couldn’t take my eyes off you, how much you reminded me of a young Smitty.”

“Really?” Will pipes up, looking proud. There’s a wetness in his eyes that Mack has to ignore for his own sake.

“Just like you,” Mack says, slipping an arm around his waist. “Suits an omega.”

Will rolls his eyes and shoves Mack because they’re always going to be immature hockey players.

“It can’t be,” Philip says, but this time his voice is soft. Mack’s heart aches with how downtrodden he sounds, how this being the best day of Mack’s adult life culminates with the worst, most devastating news for Philip—that his life is built on a lie. “I would know. I would… they would have told me.”

Will’s arm comes back up to Mack’s neck like he knows he needs the reassurance. “We don’t have to have it figured out today,” he says. Mack can hear the underlying despair, the way he doesn’t want that to be the case, and he’s only saying it for Philip. “Why don’t you take some time, talk to your—talk to your family.”

“They would have told me,” Philip insists, his eyes red and watery. If this is what Mack looks like when he’s on the verge of tears, no wonder Smitty gives him whatever he wants. Mack wants nothing more than to soothe him, to take his pain away and give it to those evil men who tear apart families. “I’m sorry you guys lost your son or whatever, but that doesn’t mean it’s me. Like, yeah, that obviously sucks, but just because I smell like you doesn’t mean it’s—that’s just crazy. I would know.”

Mack nods in acknowledgment. “Okay,” he says. He knows when to employ a tactical retreat. “Okay, let’s just—I’ll drive you back to your hotel for the night. The only thing we need from you right now is a promise that you won’t go through with the breeding program.”

Philip looks at him in shock, or worry, or something. Mack wishes he could read him easier. His scent is all over the place and no help. He stares for the longest time, getting lost in his own head before he agrees to the plan. “Yeah, of course I won’t—yeah. And, um, a ride sounds good.”

Will, despite his bad knees, is the first to open the door back out into the outside world. He looks up and down the hallways and declares it safe. “C’mon,” he says, “I’ll drive. Macky, we can get your car tomorrow. I don’t want you on the roads.”

“You’re not supposed to be driving at all,” Mack says in exasperation. He knows he’s going to lose this battle, but that doesn’t mean he’ll go down without a fight. “You’re going to set back your recovery.”

“Don’t care,” he says easily, with a cocky grin. “Worth it.”

Philip is the last to leave the equipment room, his arms back around his torso. He leaves quite a bit of space between himself and Mack and Will. But he doesn’t turn tail and run the other way. That counts for something. 

After a quick detour through the locker room to pick up their bags, they make their way together to the parking lot. Mack notices that Pims and Steve are long gone. Only then does he let out a sigh of relief.

“Which hotel do they have you at?” Will asks, changing into the alternate captain Mack knows and loves—the schmoozer, the master of small talk, the charmer. “I bet it’s the Hyatt, huh? The front office loves to put the guys up there.”

Philip nods, but he doesn’t respond. Mack watches him out of the side of his vision while they walk to Smitty’s car parked illegally in the handicapped spot out front. Well, it’s not illegal if he has his handicap decal over the rearview mirror.

Mack opens the back door for Philip on the same side as the passenger door. He watches Philip scoot into the seat and put his seatbelt on. Some old instinct rears inside him to make sure his pup is secure, safe. In another lifetime, he would be buckling his pup into a car seat to drive home from the hospital. He never got that experience. So this will have to do.

Their first car ride together as a family. Wild.

He catches his knee bouncing nervously, matched by the way Will’s driving too carefully, his every sense on alert like someone is going to come out of the woodwork to steal their pup again. Mack reaches over, uncaring if Philip sees, to thread his fingers through his alpha’s. He squeezes his hand.

“You should work on your one timers,” Mack says when no one else speaks. 

“What?” Philip says, like it’s crazy for Mack to start doling out hockey advice. “What’s wrong with my one timer?”

“Could be better,” Mack says, but he over-exaggerates his tone so Philip knows he’s teasing.

“At least I’m not slow,” he sasses back, and Mack laughs but not as loudly as Will.

“He’s got you there, old man.”

“Old man?” Mack says, balking. “You’re older than me, you idiot.”

“I have a young spirit,” Will argues with the same line of logic that he always turns to.

“Calling yourself immature doesn’t help your case,” Mack says back. 

“This is why the rookies like me and hate you,” Will shoots back, smacking on his gum. He’s not entirely wrong.

“They don’t hate me,” Mack says. “They’re intimidated by my… by my beauty.” He bats his eyelashes at Smitty like a cartoon trying to seduce a villain. 

“Sure,” Will says, too easily. He’s grinning over at Mack while they wait for a light to turn. “I’m sure that’s it.”

“He’s kinda right,” Philip speaks up. “Everyone is—I mean, he’s Macklin Celebrini, you know? That’s—that’s a big fucking deal.”

Smitty snorts. “Well, Macklin Celebrini didn’t pick up his wet towel off the floor this morning and almost killed his partner. Does that sound cool and mysterious to you, kid?”

Philip giggles, a small thing, but it’s there. God, Mack loves Will. He loves how he can ease almost any social situation. He’s so good at drawing real personalities out of people, at setting them at ease and making them feel safe. Mack squeezes Will’s fingers on the center console in quiet gratitude.

The ride to the Hyatt isn’t long. That’s the main reason they usually choose that hotel. Will drives through the circle by the front entrance and puts the car in park. Neither Mack nor Will are eager for the trip to end. Philip doesn’t get out immediately. He stays seated, like he too doesn’t want to leave them.

It’s that theory that makes Mack speak up. His pup doesn’t want to leave them either, but he’s also not ready to face everything. He doesn’t believe them yet, and Mack can’t blame him. It’s a lot to wrap a mind around. 

They’ve been patient for eighteen years. They can be patient for a bit longer. At least they know he’s safe. They can watch out for him now.

“We’ll be—” Mack looks at Will, knowing they’re on the same page. “We’re teammates, whether what we think is true or not. We can just be—we’ll be teammates. I’m your captain. Smitty is your alternate, but he’s on the LTIR. We can just be that for now, okay? No pressure or anything from us.”

Philip looks so lost, like a little lamb who traveled too far from the herd. It breaks Mack’s heart, but then the sight of him being here at all heals it back together. 

His son takes a deep breath, nods, and gets out of the car, hockey bag hitting the ground carelessly. “See you tomorrow, captain.”

Mack and Will watch him disappear inside. 

-

Back at their house, the evening is quiet. Mack’s deep in his own head, going through the motions of a night in. Will orders something that arrives too quickly. Mack shoves Will’s medication at him, knowing he needs to take it since he went too hard today. When he swallows the pills, Mack thanks him with a kiss on the cheek, and then he gets out his ice packs to help with the inflammation. He mentally checks the list of things their medical people have said about Will’s knee recovery. He thinks he’s covered everything.

Later, when dinner is long over, Mack ices his sore quad while they watch TV before bed. Smitty sets about his bedtime routine with a precision that belies his stormy thoughts, stumbling with the crutches that he’s supposed to be using all the time and not just when Mack whines at him.

After this long together, Mack has come to terms with the fact that Will processes things differently—he needs time to think about every angle of something before he’s ready to make any kind of move. He’s a planner, through and through. Mack knows he needs time and sometimes space, but this isn’t something he can contain for much longer. 

“Why would they approach him so early?” Mack asks over the bad action movie taking place on the screen. He can’t hold his thoughts in any longer. He needs to talk about it.

Will snorts like the answer is obvious. “Look at his pedigree.” Smitty has this crazy theory that he won’t let go of. “They can’t wait until he’s thirty to get their hands on the grandson of Sidney Crosby, the son of Macklin Celebrini. The bloodline goes crazy.”

Mack rolls his eyes. He’s not Sidney Crosby’s missing pup, no matter what Will thinks. They’ve never discussed it with Sid, but how could Sid not bring it up if it were true? Mack has been in those shoes, trying to get his pup back. If it were true, Sid would have mentioned it the first time they played together, when they trained together for Four Nations or the Olympics. It would have come up.

“I’m sure they were hoping they could talk him into it before you could intervene,” Will says, curling his arm around Mack as he settles into their preferred sleeping position. “You should call Sid in the morning, see if he knows anything about this.”

Sid’s long been retired, but he has connections all over the league. He’ll want to know they’re starting this shit again. He’ll be heated. “Yeah, okay,” Mack agrees. “He can take up the fight with the NHLPA. I’m just—I’m too tired.”

“I know you are, old man,” Will says, placing a kiss on Mack’s temple.

“What if I hadn’t been there?” Mack asks. This is the spiral he’s been avoiding all day. “What if they tried that when I wasn’t there, when no one would have thought twice about the omega rights org wanting to talk to the new omega on the team? I—it makes me sick to think about it.”

“So, don’t think about it,” Will says, easy as anything. “You were there. You did stop them. You found him, Macky. You protected our pup when I couldn’t. You—you did so good, baby.”

Mack lets out a shuddering breath. “He’s so good, Will. He’s—he’s an amazing player. I haven’t been this excited to play with someone since… since you, I think.”

Will cuddles in closer to him, the presence of his body making Mack feel secure, safe, in the way that only his alpha can. “I can’t wait to get better and be out there with you guys.”

A comfortable silence wraps around them. Mack can’t be silent like this with anyone other than Will. He stares at the television, taking nothing in, while his brain continues to spin the entire day around like a microwave. He knows it’ll take time for Philip to come around, and maybe he’ll never accept them as his parents. That would be a hard pill to swallow, but Mack knows at least he’ll have some kind of relationship with him—as a teammate, if nothing more.

He runs through the whole day again from beginning to end, turning it all over. There’s one thing he keeps coming back to.

“I can’t believe they named him Philip,” Mack says, not holding back his disgust.

-

The rest of training camp, Mack keeps his distance. He interacts only as much as he should as the captain and as someone who Pims assigns to play with Philip on the power play. It’s awkward at first, playing with someone that young and fast. Mack hates to admit Smitty’s right in any capacity, but he has slowed down just a hair. He’s never been the one struggling to keep up. He’s always been the play driver.

Just because he’s keeping his distance, doesn’t mean Mack isn’t watching Philip. He interacts well in the room and on the ice. By the end of the first week, he has the team wrapped around his finger without seeming to even try. Mack, most of all. 

No one comments on their similar scents or looks. Whether that be because they’re dumb hockey players who are unobservant, or because they’re mostly all dumb betas who are noseblind, Mack takes the W for what it is.

By the first pre-season game, it’s more than obvious that Philip is here to stay on the Sharks roster. The general manager, two removed from the one who first welcomed him to the team, calls Mack directly to ask him to take Philip under his wing. It’s not unheard of for Mack and Will to offer their guest house to a rookie who shows great promise. Management likes to have the rookies with a steady couple who aren’t known for doing anything too crazy—oh how the times have changed.

Mack talks to Will about it, how they’d approach it and what their ground rules would be. They both have to acknowledge that there’s a chance Philip says no anyway.

With their plan in place, Mack and Will arrive for the first pre-season game at home. Neither of them are playing, Will because of his knees and Mack to give the other players a chance to show what they can do. He’ll be watching with Will in the owner’s box. 

Before that, though, they make an appearance in the locker room. Mack has a captain’s speech to give, and Will has connections to make since he hasn’t been there for camp other than the few times he came in for his physical therapy.

Will clocks it first. Mack feels a soft hand on his arm and follows Will’s gaze. Philip sits at his stall, his face down and pale in a way that Mack has never seen him.

Is it nerves? He looks at Will to share this thought, and Will shakes his head and raises his eyebrows. Philip didn’t seem like the kind of rookie who gets nervous like this. Will bats Mack toward him with a go talk to him look in his eyes.

“Hey, kid,” Mack says, perching on the bench next to Philip. “You okay? Nervous for the game?”

“What?” Philip’s whole body twitches like he wasn’t expecting anyone in this loud locker room to approach him. Mack watches him register who’s talking to him. To Mack’s delight, he seems to settle down quickly, like his presence is a good thing.

“I asked if you’re okay,” Mack says as patiently as he can. “No offense, but you look kinda… not okay.”

Philip’s mouth twitches in amusement. Mack has started to categorize his expressions over the course of training camp. He’s an expressive kid, never learned to hide anything like the Smith side of the family. 

“I finally talked to my par—to my family,” he says, keeping his eyes on the floor. “Did you know that the contract was complete after I turned eighteen and got drafted? Don’t worry though, because they got their bonus for getting me to the NHL.” His voice turns abrasively sarcastic in a way that Mack has come to learn is uncharacteristic of him. 

Philip looks over at Mack, his face full of fire and rage. His hands shake at his sides, having not learned to cover that by taping and re-taping his stick like Mack does. “Can you believe that? Can you—”

Mack can’t stand it any longer. His omega heart can’t keep doing this, he pulls Philip into his arms at an awkward angle for how they’re sitting. “No, I really can’t. I can’t—I can’t imagine not wanting you. I’ve spent my entire life wanting you, Philip.”

Philip takes a deep shuddering breath into Mack’s neck, breathing his scent in deeply for the first time. It’s—they should have had this when Philip was a pup—his omega father’s scent calming him down. 

“They’re grifters,” Philip says. “They—none of my siblings are really mine either. They’re someone else’s stolen pups too. I have to tell them at some point, I just don’t want to have that conversation at all.”

“We can talk about it later,” Mack says, noticing for the first time that the locker room is quiet. He looks over at Will who isn’t even being subtle about watching them. “Let’s get through this game and then we can talk more, okay?”

“The game, yeah,” Philip says, pulling back and taking another breath. 

“Smitty and I wanted to—well,” Mack says, scratching at the back of his neck like Will does to calm him down. It doesn’t work as well when he does it to himself. “We’d do it for any rookie who needed a place, but we have a guest apartment at our house, completely separate from the main house. We wanted to offer it to you for the season. It’s something we’ve done before for rookies, so it’s not—but we want you there. We want you close. I want to get to know you. Is that okay?”

Will causes some disruption around them, maybe starting the captain’s speech he has memorized by now, always picking up Mack’s slack. Through the commotion, Mack keeps going, talking directly into Philip’s ear, “You don’t have to give an answer now, just… I can’t stand seeing you like this. Your family does want you. Your family loves you so much, kid.”

“Mack,” Philip says, eyes watery but not dripping with tears. He’s a strong kid. “Yeah, I want to stay with you guys. If you don’t mind too much.”

Mack feels himself grin so big that it hurts his mouth. “Absolutely.”

“Let’s show Anaheim that they ain’t shit!” Will says in the background. Cheers rise up, the boys hooting and hollering. 

Mack gives Philip one more pat on the back and stands. “We’ll talk after the game. You can come back with us and see the apartment for yourself. Get settled in.”

“Mack,” Philip says, drawing him back in. “What was… what would my name have been? If they hadn’t… you know. What were you going to name me?”

“We hadn’t officially decided,” Mack says, thinking back on how he felt staring down at his newborn lying on his chest, before the world went to hell, “but I was about to give in. You would have been another William Smith, the like fifteenth or whatever, with some horrendous nickname. I might have talked him into Celebrini-Smith.”

Philip lets out a painful laugh. He nods, and he stands up. “Thanks, Mack.”

“You shouldn’t thank me for almost saddling you with a horrible name,” Mack says. “The labor was hard, and I was exhausted. That’s the only way Smitty was going to win.”

“Why didn’t you ever…” Philip looks around the room and sees they still have relative privacy. “You never had other kids?”

Mack feels his face freeze. “I couldn’t… after you, I couldn’t bear the thought of—I couldn’t replace you. I couldn’t—and thinking there was a chance they stole another baby? I would have ended it, Philip. Not even Smitty could have pulled me from—”

“Hey,” Philip says, rubbing a hand on Mack’s arm, an omega gesture to calm another omega. “One day I want to hear all about it.”

Mack nods, trying and failing to smile. “I’m just so glad it won’t happen to you. You’re safe from that. And they’ll never take you from me again.”

“Never,” Philip repeats. 

“You better go,” Mack says, nodding toward the lineup with the rest of the dressed players. “Kick some ass for me.”

Philip shoots back Will’s cocky grin. “Always.”

-

“I don’t remember that part of the speech,” Mack says to Will when he sidles up next to him in the owner’s box later. “What did you say? ‘Anaheim ain’t shit?’”

“It’s called improvising, Mack, you wouldn’t get it,” Smitty says with a badly suppressed smile. “He okay?”

“Yes and no,” Mack says. “We’ll talk after.”

The Sharks win the game on Philip’s wrister in the third period. The three of them pile into Will’s overpriced SUV and drive home together. For the first time since he was a teenager himself, Mack feels complete.

He feels right.

Here’s his family. 

“You finally ready for another one?” Will asks after they’ve gone to bed. 

“After the season,” Mack says. “After we’ve won a Cup with our pup and I’m retired. Then you can mate me and knock me up again.”

“It’s a deal.” Will rolls over onto Mack, placing teasing kisses all over his neck that make Mack laugh. “But we should start practicing.”
